During the Japanese occupation, the Imperial Japanese Army abducted an estimated total of 200,000 young girls from Korea, Taiwan, China, Indonesia, and the Philippines. In the Philippines, it is estimated that 1,000 women were forcibly seized by Japanese soldiers to serve as comfort women

The use of udon or odong noodles in Baguio, Davao, and other parts of Mindanao is another Japanese influence, thanks to "Japanese [migrant] workers, some of whom had helped build the Kennon Road in Baguio City at the turn of the century, who went to work in [Davao's] abaca (hemp) plantations." Davao has a version of lasagna called haob na odong which uses udon noodles. 

The use of miso in sinigang sa miso is indubitably Japanese. The use of vetsin, specifically the Ajinomoto brand, is clearly Japanese.

Ilocanos also have dried seaweed called gamet processed into black sheets. It is considered the “black gold.” Every sheet costs an arm and a leg so, not surprisingly, Ilocanos use it sparingly, adding it to scrambled eggs the way the French do with truffles, to flavor and also to upgrade an ordinary dish. 

Halo-halo is the quintessential Philippine dessert. It is one of the most popular meriendas for Filipinos. The recipe for halo-halo varies, but the key ingredients are shaved ice, milk, and fruits. Some of the commonly added ingredients are macapuno, red beans, jackfruit, and corn (Villaluz). Halo means a mixture (noun) or to mix (verb: ihalo) in Filipino. 

Thus, halo-halo refers not only to the design of this Filipino dessert but also to the manner of eating it. Mongo con hielo, or red bean preserves on top of finely shaved ice, is said to be a Japanese influence. So is the practice of preserving two other beans in syrup: garbanzos and kidney beans.(5) The halo-halo is therefore basically inspired by the Japanese shaved ice dessert called kakigori.

To understand why halo-halo is the Filipino ice dessert, one must navigate through the Philippines’ complex history.The Japanese community in Manila prevailed until World War II. The Japanese who initially emigrated to the Philippines were Christians, merchants, un- or semi-skilled workers, and soldiers. An inspection of the Japanese settlements in 1606 concluded that there were a total of “91 shops and lodging” in Dilao. The Japanese also settled in San Miguel and Cavite. It was estimated that the number of Japanese settlers peaked at about 3,000 in the 1620s and dropped to 800 in 1637. The Japanese did not fully assimilate to the society in the Philippines. They continued to practice their culture by speaking Japanese, wearing traditional clothes, and eating Japanese cuisine. Over time, some Japanese became micro-entrepreneurs selling refreshments, garments, shoes, and other products). The rise of the Japanese commercial sector is linked to the brothel business run by some of the Japanese.

Refreshment parlors, restaurants, and shops were established in Sampaloc – the red-light district of Manila until 1918. Some of these commercial establishments served as a prostitution front. A census carried out by the US revealed that there were almost 200 Japanese prostitutes in 1910. In addition, 17% of the American soldiers in Manila between 1910 and 1917 had venereal disease.. The high incidence of sexually transmitted diseases prompted the American officials to close down the brothels.

Ice products were becoming more popular in the public. Clarke’s was the first American-owned ice cream parlor to grace Escolta in 1898. Ice cream became more easily accessible and people who could afford it obsessed over it. Japanese entrepreneurs in Manila seized the opportunity to compete in the expanding ice market. Felice Prudente Sta. Maria, a Filipino historian, claims that it was actually the production of soft drinks and Japanese desserts that elicited mass demand for ice in the country

Those who profited from the illegal business in Sampaloc set up their own shops and restaurants elsewhere in the city.It is important to note, however, that there were some legitimate Japanese commercial establishments in Manila that were patronized by both the Americans and the Filipinos. Filipino historian Ambeth Ocampo argues that halo-halo originated from kakigori – a shaved ice dessert topped with anko (sweet red bean paste), condensed milk, and flavored syrup.
