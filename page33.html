AMERICAN ERA: 
FOOD AND CUISINE

Foods like ice cream, donuts, hamburgers, fries, sandwiches, cakes, hotdogs and cookies were also introduced by the Americans.

Food played an important role in Americans’ evaluation of the Philippines’ modernity and readiness for independence.
In 1900, American Edith Moses gave a “Filipina Lunch party” at her home in Manila. The luncheon featured neither dishes nor dining customs native to the Philippines, yet Moses considered it a great success.
Americans portrayed Filipinos as people who took hospitality seriously and who would be offended if offered food was not consumed.
 A 1906 menu from the Wannamaker Department Store’s Tea Room in Philadelphia, for example, featured croquettes, salads, eggs, and many pastries, cakes, and ice cream dishes but none of the chops or roasted meats that would feature in restaurants catering to men (Wannamaker Menu, 1906). Moses offered her guests the taste of America, in both the aesthetic and the physiological senses of the word.
