SPANISH ERA: EDUCATION

During the Spanish reign, they established Catholic-run schools. Friars and nuns were the teachers at these schools. 

Spain introduced the European system of education in the Philippines.The Filipino people were literate before the Spanish ever arrived, but the Spanish added new subjects to their academia such as math, Spanish, and business. In time, the Spanish also set up colleges (segregated by gender).

SYSTE OF EDUCATION

Although a systematic and institutionalized kind of education was established, unfortunately there was still inequality in attaining education. The system of education familiarized by the Filipinos was religious and patriarchal. People were also taught that social mobility was achieved through education, but sadly this manifested itself in social inequality and female subordination.

The higher priority for educational attainment was placed on men rather than on women. Although the Mestizos and wealthy people enjoyed the privileges of entering prestigious schools, there were women only vocational schools for women. However, most women were denied their rights to education due to the patriarchal belief that women should stay only at home.

Only wealthy Filipinos or the Illustrados were accommodated by the schools.

SYSTEM OF WRITING

The system of writing during the Spanish Regime was Spanish Alphabet. Baybayin script was replaced since using this gave the indigenous people more leverage dealing with the local Spanish colonial administrators. The Baybayin script was neglected and was not used by succeeding generations.

EDUCATIONAL DECREE OF 1863

It provided for the establishment of at least two free primary schools, one for boys and another for girls, in each town under the responsibility of the municipal government. It also commended the creation of a free public normal school to train men as teachers, supervised by the Jesuits.

UNIVERSITIES


Colegio de Santa Potenciana 

First school and college for girls that opened in the Philippines, in 1589.

Universidad de San Felipe de Austria

The first public university in the Philippines that was established on December 23, 1640. It closed down in 1643.

Universidad de San Ignacio

A university in the city of Manila which existed in the Spanish Philippines. It was founded in 1590 and is one of the earliest educational institutions built by Europeans in East Asia when it was established by Spanish Jesuits.

University of Santo Tomas

Initially named as the Colegio de Nuestra Señora del Santísimo Rosario. Founded on April 28, 1611, by Miguel de Benavides, Archbishop of Manila, it has the oldest extant university charter in the Philippines and in Asia, and is one of the world's largest Catholic universities in terms of enrollment found on one campus.

Ateneo de Manila University

The Jesuits also founded the Colegio de San José (former name) in 1601 and took over the management of a school that became the Escuela Municipal in 1859, later renamed Ateneo Municipal de Manila in 1865, now the Ateneo de Manila University.

Colegio de San Juan de Letrán

The college was founded by Don Juan Geromino Guerrero in 1620, a retired Spanish officer and one of the Knights of Malta, in Intramuros as Colegio de Niños Huérfanos de San Juan de Letrán. The school was intended to educate and mold orphans to be good Christian citizens.

Colegio de Santa Isabel

It was opened in 1632. The sole purpose of this was to provide education for orphaned girls who could not afford to educate themselves. The lessons taught were basically about household tasks such as cooking, embroidery-making, sewing and others skills necessary for good housekeeping.
